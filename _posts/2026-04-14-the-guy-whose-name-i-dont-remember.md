We all grew up hearing *don't take candy from strangers.*

Nobody ever told me the other version.

## Vegas, 2018

A little setup so the rest makes sense. I spent roughly seven years as a university lecturer at [DigiPen](https://www.digipen.edu), the computer science and game development school I actually graduated from. Two years teaching continuing education in Singapore, then five years teaching the first half of the undergrad program in South Korea. I'm not a professor — I don't have a PhD, my title was "Lecturer," and I'm careful to say that. I taught C, C++, game engine architecture, and enough data structures to give college freshmen a reasonable chance at not hating themselves during their first internship.

During those seven years I also shipped a mobile game for a friend, built a handful of game engines from scratch, and [co-authored a book on game development patterns](https://www.amazon.com/Game-Development-Patterns-Best-Practices-ebook/dp/B01MRP7SPA/). So when I decided I was done with teaching and wanted to get back into building things full-time, I thought my resume would read like "seven years of senior-ish engineering work with a teaching layer on top."

Turns out "Lecturer, DigiPen, 7 years" on a resume reads like "has never had a real job." I was ready to start at a higher level than the industry was ready to start me at. Which — honestly, fair. I hadn't shipped a commercial product in a while, and hiring managers don't have time to pick apart the nuance between "side projects and a book" and "real production experience." So I came in at the junior level, rocked that job, and a year and a half later ended up at a sports betting company in Vegas.

The betting job was fine, but the Nevada Gaming Commission regulates client-side code *heavily* — every mobile app change has to get reviewed for potential exploits. Which means iteration is slow, and slow iteration is not why I got into this. I started looking around.

That's when I applied to Meta for the first time.

## The Party

Around the same time I was going through the Meta interview loop, my wife and I went to some kind of members' event at our gym. The kind of thing where a handful of couples stand around with plastic cups and make polite conversation. My wife had a friend at the gym, and her friend's husband was there — older than us, a programmer at some local company, the kind of guy who'd been doing this for a long time.

At some point the conversation drifted to what I was up to, and I mentioned I was applying at Meta.

He shook his head immediately.

*"Oh, you don't want to work there. You'll have to move to the Bay Area. It's so expensive to live there."*

He said it with the kind of certainty that makes you reflexively want to agree.

And here's the thing: he wasn't *wrong* about the Bay Area being expensive. That part is true. Rent is insane, groceries are insane, parking is insane, everything there is more expensive than the same thing literally anywhere else. If you were weighing a Bay Area job against a Vegas job purely on cost of living, yeah, it looks grim.

What he was missing — completely, utterly, with no indication he even knew it was a variable — was how Meta pays engineers. He had no idea what a new grad at a big tech company makes. No idea what a senior makes. No idea that the compensation at these companies is scaled, pretty deliberately, to the cost of living in the places they hire. He wasn't telling me "Meta pays X and Bay Area rent is Y, so the math looks like Z." He was telling me "San Francisco is expensive, therefore don't go."

He also wasn't asking me what I was looking for, or what stage of my career I was at, or whether I had kids, or whether I wanted to travel, or any of the actual inputs to a decision that personal. He was just confidently giving advice about a field he didn't know the numbers in, to a guy he'd met twenty minutes earlier.

I listened politely, nodded, said something non-committal, and went home.

I didn't listen to him.

## The Other Version of the Warning

Here's the thing that always stuck with me: I was around 35 at the time. I'd been out of school for years. I was pretty confident I could think for myself. And *even so*, I remember the little flicker of doubt after that conversation — the "huh, maybe this guy knows something I don't" feeling that creeps in when someone says something with total conviction and you can't immediately counter it.

I imagine a version of me who was 22 instead of mid-30s, hearing that same line, and just... taking it at face value. *"Oh yeah, I guess I shouldn't apply there. That guy sounded like he knew what he was talking about."* I'd never know what I'd missed.

We all grew up with *don't take candy from strangers.* It's drilled into you as a kid. It's sensible, it's specific, it's easy to remember.

Nobody ever told me the other version: **don't take advice from strangers.** Especially confident ones. Especially when they have no basis and no skin in your outcome. The candy at least might give you a stomachache. The advice can redirect your whole career.

I'm not saying every older programmer at a gym party is wrong. Plenty of people give genuinely great advice. My point is that confidence and correctness are almost unrelated in casual conversation, and the person most willing to tell you with absolute certainty what you shouldn't do is usually also the person who hasn't done the work to know.

## What Actually Happened

I didn't get the job that first time. I made it all the way through the loop and failed at the final interview — the signal wasn't strong enough, as Meta puts it. That's fine. The interview loop is long, and the last round is the hardest, and people wash out there all the time. I went back to the sports betting job, kept building stuff, kept getting better.

I reapplied in 2020. That time I made it.

And here's a small twist: I didn't even end up in the Bay Area. I ended up in Seattle, which Meta adjusts down slightly from San Francisco. Technically it's not a cost-of-living adjustment either — it's a **cost-of-labor** adjustment. The Bay Area pays more because more companies compete for engineers there; Seattle is still high because Amazon, Microsoft, and a dozen others compete too, just slightly less fiercely. Back when I joined in 2020, the Bay Area was something like 10% above Seattle for the same level. Still tuned to the market. Still way more than I was making in Vegas. The gym guy's "San Francisco is expensive" warning didn't even apply to where I actually moved, and if it had, Meta would have priced that in too.

Somewhere in the middle of onboarding, when I finally saw the actual numbers and the actual cost-of-living adjustments and the actual "how much of my salary is left over after rent" math, I remembered the guy at the gym. With his certainty. And his absolute cluelessness about the thing he was so confident about.

If I'd listened to him, I wouldn't be at Meta. I wouldn't have this career. I wouldn't be [building a terminal emulator in Rust in my free time](/2026/04/13/vim-can-wait/) with the kind of professional stability that lets me do that. I'd be somewhere, probably fine, telling *my* version of the story about the job I almost applied for but the guy at the gym said not to.

I don't even remember his name.

But I will never forget him.

## The Rule I Actually Use

A few things I've held onto from that night:

1. **Confidence ≠ correctness.** The person who sounds the most certain is not necessarily the person who's done the most work. Usually they're the person who's done *just enough* to form an opinion and *not enough* to know the edges of it.
2. **Ask the person's skin in the game.** Not literally — that's rude — but mentally. Is this person currently working in the domain they're giving advice about? Have they ever been? Do they know the numbers? If the answer is no, weight the advice accordingly.
3. **Beware of "you don't want to do that" without a "because."** Any version of "you don't want to do X" that doesn't immediately present evidence is a vibe, not advice. Vibes are fine to collect. Vibes are not fine to base a career move on.
4. **Listen to the people who actually did the thing.** If I'd asked a Meta engineer about Meta, I'd have gotten a radically different answer than the guy at the gym gave me. Those are the people you should be asking. Not the ones who have opinions about the thing. The ones who've lived it.

None of this is news. It's the kind of advice people nod at and forget. But I think about the gym guy often. Not with any bitterness — he probably meant well. Just as a reminder that somewhere out there, right now, a 22-year-old is getting a confident dose of bad advice from someone who has no idea what they're talking about, and they might actually take it.

If that's you: don't.

Get the numbers. Ask the people doing the work. Apply anyway.

Don't take candy from strangers. Don't take advice from them either.
